Metabolic interplay drives population cycles in a cross-feeding microbial community

Answers to the reading theory exercise

Make sure you have read your handout (or download the handout here) for the summary of this paper.

Question 1: What is the exciting result about mutualisms from this research?

The exciting results are twofold. One, it is usually assumed that mutualisms may lead to population explosion of both mutualists. But the authors show mutualisms can drive population cycles that can lead to stable coexistence. Two, it is often thought that mutualisms are susceptible to cheaters. The authors show that in this case, due to the cycling, the mutualism prevents invasion of cheaters.

Question 2: Can you apply that result to any mutualism of interest? Why or why not?

Scenarios where the results may apply: bacterial communities where cross-feeding mutualisms are common or other communities where a third resource limits the growth of the community and prevents a population explosion. Scenarios where they may not apply: communities where the third limiting resource is not present. Further, communities where the cycling does not emerge might be susceptible to a cheater.

Question 3: The researchers had already demonstrated the existence of cycles through their experiments. What additional value did the model add?

The model helped them figure out the specific mechanism that causes the cycles. The authors used the model to reduce the complexity in a stepwise fashion, stripping away details until they arrived at a model with the essential mechanism that led to the cycles. The model also allowed them to investigate a range of scenarios for which experiments were not done, such as investigating whether a cheater can invade the community.

Question 4: Bonus question: why does the mutualism not result in a population explosion from positive feedback between both species?

Both mutualists are limited by glucose, a resource they both compete for. This prevents runaway population growth in both strains.
