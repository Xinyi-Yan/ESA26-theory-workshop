Effective leadership and decision-making in animal groups on the move

Answers to the reading theory exercise

Make sure you have read your handout (or download the handout here) for the summary of this paper.

Question 1: In this model, can uninformed individuals distinguish between other uninformed individuals and informed individuals? If you aren’t sure, which assumption would make the results stronger?

This model assumes that fish cannot distinguish between informed and uninformed individuals. Their social interaction with either types is the same. If uninformed individuals can distinguish informed individuals and be more influenced by their directions (i.e. in addition to social interactions as before, they also try to align with the preferred direction of the informed individuals), this effectively would be a group with more informed individuals. Therefore, group navigation accuracy would increase when this assumption was reversed, but other results should be qualitatively the same.

Question 2: In the scenarios where two informed groups have different preferred directions, what do you think would happen if the informed fish could adjust their behaviors, e.g. they put more weight on their preferred direction when the group is traveling in a similar direction and put less weight on their preference when it deviates from the group’s decision?

The authors explore this situation in the paper and found that “Such a feedback loop allows consensus decisions (as opposed to averaging) for smaller differences in preferred direction.”

Question 3: Are there other systems where these assumptions might fit well enough for us to apply the insights here to their group decision making?

Migrating land animals and birds, but in some of those systems group members may be able to recognize informed individuals

Question 4: What are some other fish behaviors in real life that are not included in this model? How would they potentially influence the group decision making?

This is an open-ended question depending on the system.
